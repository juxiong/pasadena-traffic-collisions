Motivation

The Average American spends 38 hours per year sitting still in traffic and highway congestion costs over $160 billion nationally. This includes direct costs such as the cost of fuel and indirect costs such as the cost attributed to business losses. Understanding intersections is equally as important, as demonstrated in the visual just below. Our goal for this project was to analyze traffic collision and traffic volume data to create visualizations and models that help us find the most important contributing factors to an accident, specifically in Pasadena. Since about 20% of traffic congestion costs in the US are coming from Los Angeles alone, Pasadena is a good place to start.

Importance of Intersections

Pasadena Open Data

The data we used was taken from Pasadenaâ€™s Open Data Portal and records over 17,000 accidents in Pasadena over the past ten years. For each accidents, there are over 55 features, each containing details of that accident. This data was used to create the visualizations and train the model.

Speaking with City of Pasadena

We spoke to Traffic Engineers for the City of Pasadena about our project. They described an interesting direction to this project that they had not investigated but were curious about; they provided the data for the volume of traffic in Pasadena and asked us to examine collision rates in relation to the volume of traffic.

Model Building

In the traffic collision data, there are many unknown causes for accidents. So, we have created a model that predicts unknown values for cause of accident.

Visualizations

Traffic Volume

We plotted the volume of traffic measured in Pasadena. The volume data is essential for comparing accident rate and traffic rate.

Collision Analysis

After aggregating collision data in Pasadena, we found that the number of accidents involving pedestrians or cyclists were higher around the times 12 pm to 10 pm. The numbers were specifically high around 7 pm to 10 pm with around 300 accidents involving pedestrians and cyclists. This suggests that poor lighting conditions may have had something to do with the high collision rates with pedestrians and cyclists around this time.

XGBoost Multi Classification

We trained a model using the Pasadena Open Portal data with an XGBoost multi classification model. When testing the trained model to use it to determine between 3 potential causes of an accident, we obtained an accuracy score of 92%. We were also able to determine the most important features (as ranked by the XGBoost model) that determine the cause of an accident, where the causes were Traffic Signals and Signs, R/W Violation, and Unsafe Speed. We show these results below. The two most important contributing factors to determining the cause of an accident seemed to be the movement of the first party and the movement of the second party. This makes sense because this would allow the model to distinguish between the type of accident that could have happened, which would help determine the cause of the accident.
